Latter-day Saints Temple

What is the purpose of these temples?

In The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, temples are considered the most sacred places of worship, serving a distinct purpose from local meetinghouses. While meetinghouses are open to the public for weekly Sunday services, temples are reserved for members who have met specific requirements to enter. Within these walls, members participate in sacred ceremonies known as ordinances, such as the endowment, which provides spiritual instruction and covenants, and sealings, where couples are married for "time and all eternity," reflecting the belief that family relationships can continue beyond death. Beyond these individual and family ceremonies, temples are central to the Church’s mission of service and connection to ancestors. Members perform proxy baptisms and other ordinances for deceased individuals, offering them the same opportunity to accept the gospel of Jesus Christ. Ultimately, members view the temple as a place to find peace, seek guidance, and focus on their relationship with God and the eternal nature of their families.

How are they used?

Temples are used for sacred ordinances, which are specific religious ceremonies. Members prepare for this by meeting with local leaders to receive a "recommend," which grants them entry. Once inside, they participate in the endowment, where they receive religious instruction and make sacred promises; sealings, where couples are married for time and eternity; and the performance of proxy ordinances, such as baptisms, for deceased ancestors to offer them the opportunity to accept the faith.
